Study of the Domain

Idea

Legendary creatures have been a vital part of human culture and imagination, embodying humanity's deepest fears, hopes, and mysteries. From ancient myths to modern narratives, these mythical beings transcend their origins, becoming cultural symbols that reveal the beliefs, values, and artistic expressions of the societies that created them. The allure of legendary creatures lies in their ability to blur the line between the known and the unknown, the real and the fantastical, offering both cautionary tales and sources of wonder.

These creatures are not confined to any one medium or period; they evolve alongside human creativity, appearing in literature, visual art, music, and interactive media. Each iteration reflects the cultural context of its time, revealing insights into the society that embraced it. For instance, dragons, which appear in legends across Europe, Asia, and the Americas, have been portrayed variously as symbols of power, greed, wisdom, or chaos, depending on the cultural framework. Similarly, the vampire has transitioned from folklore's grotesque revenant to literature's romantic antihero, illustrating how legendary creatures adapt to changing societal narratives.

Our project focuses on the enduring fascination with these mythical beings by exploring their representation in a variety of forms and contexts. The chosen domain spans both historical and contemporary interpretations, showcasing how legendary creatures function as enduring cultural artifacts. From Renaissance art to modern video games, and from ancient Japanese prints to 20th-century cinema, these representations highlight the versatility of legendary creatures in reflecting human creativity and cultural shifts.

The items selected for this project were chosen to provide a comprehensive view of the domain, illustrating its richness and diversity. Each item not only depicts a legendary creature but also offers a unique perspective on the cultural, historical, and artistic significance of these beings. By studying these items, we aim to uncover the deeper meanings behind the representations of legendary creatures and understand how they contribute to cultural identity and knowledge transmission.

This investigation is particularly relevant in the context of digital humanities, where the convergence of traditional scholarship and digital tools enables us to analyze and reinterpret cultural phenomena. Through this project, we aspire to demonstrate the role of legendary creatures in shaping and reflecting human culture, offering insights that bridge past and present, myth and reality.

Theoretical Model

At this stage, we sought additional potential connections to enhance the initial model. This involved researching more information about agents, places, and dates associated with the main items and integrating these new data points into the model. We used various colors to highlight to which set of concepts each entity belongs to:

  • what: red
  • who: blue
  • where: green
  • when: yellow

Additionally, we opted to reshape some item entities using the FRBR conceptual model for a more structured representation.

Conceptual Model

The final step of the knowledge organization phase involved creating a conceptual model using an ontological approach. To achieve this, we refactored the terms from the theoretical model by aligning them with terms from existing schemas, vocabularies, and ontologies. Specifically, we reused the following:

  • aat: http://vocab.getty.edu/aat/
  • cidoc: http://www.cidoc-crm.org/cidoc-crm/
  • dbo: http://dbpedia.org/ontology/
  • dcterms: https://www.dublincore.org/specifications/dublin-core/dcmi-terms/
  • edm: http://www.europeana.eu/schemas/edm/
  • fo: https://semantics.id/ns/example/film/
  • foaf: http://xmlns.com/foaf/0.1/
  • geo: http://www.opengis.net/ont/geosparql#
  • lrmoo: http://www.cidoc-crm.org/lrmoo/
  • owl: http://www.w3.org/2002/07/owl#
  • prov: http://www.w3.org/ns/prov#
  • rdfs: https://www.w3.org/2000/01/rdf-schema#
  • schema: https://schema.org/
  • skos: http://www.w3.org/2004/02/skos/core#
  • wikidata: http://www.wikidata.org/entity/

Among the various options for formalizing the FRBR conceptual model, we chose LRMoo due to its full compatibility with CIDOC CRM. This decision ensured consistency with our overarching ontological framework. Additionally, we used owl:sameAs to link entities to their corresponding VIAF entries and to establish equivalences between classes in different ontologies.

To further enrich our model, we identified semantic associations between entities or classes, employing various SKOS properties. Specifically:

  • skos:broadMatch: Used to link a class from a specific ontology to one from another ontology that represents a broader concept (e.g., the class Legendary Creatures is broadly matched with the CIDOC class E28 Conceptual Object).
  • skos:narrowMatch: Used to link a class from a specific ontology to one from another ontology that represents a narrower concept (e.g., the schema class Organization is narrowly matched with the CIDOC class E39 Actor).
  • skos:related: Used to link two entities that are connected in some way, without indicating a hierarchical relationship (e.g., the occupation Composer is related to the occupation Conductor, which is also related to the occupation Pianist).

These steps allowed us to formalize and enrich our conceptual model, ensuring it was both robust and interoperable across various ontological frameworks.

Knowledge Representation

Items Description

Once the modeling activity was completed, we revisited the items to describe them based on the conceptual model we designed. To achieve this, we wrote a python script that automatically extracted all the triples from the yED diagram and stored them in a CSV file. The script then filtered the triples, retaining only those where our items appeared as the subject or object. Next, it grouped the triples by the specific item they referred to and generated a separate CSV file for each group. In these files, columns correspond to the triples' subject, predicate, and object, while rows represent the individual statements.

RDF Production

A specific URI was defined for each item, and another Python script was used to create a comprehensive RDF dataset from the main CSV file generated from the yED diagram. For demonstration purposes, we also created a smaller dataset using the CSV files specifically related to the items. This approach improved the readability of the knowledge graph visualization.

Text Processing

XML/TEI encoding

To meet the requirements for encoding a text in XML/TEI, we selected the Italian translation of Monstrorum Historia, focusing specifically on the first section, "Definizione ed etimologia," from Chapter 3, "Il mostro in generale." This sample provided an excellent opportunity to test a variety of encoding practices, including the identification of people and places, the representation of different languages (such as Ancient Greek, Latin, and Hebrew), and the inclusion of bibliographic references.
